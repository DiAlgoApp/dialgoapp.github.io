Telecare vs Family communication apps: Which does your relative need?

We compare traditional telecare alarm services with family communication and coordination apps for older adults care.

When an older adult or a dependent relative lives alone or spends many hours without company, families look for technological solutions to ensure their safety and well-being. Two of the most common options are traditional telecare (the classic red panic button) and family communication mobile apps.

Although they are often considered alternatives, they address different needs and, in many cases, serve as complementary tools. In this guide, we analyze both options in detail to help you decide which one is right for your relative.

1. Traditional telecare: Immediate response to emergencies

Telecare consists of a physical device (usually a pendant or wristband with a red button) connected to a telephone line. Pressing the button instantly connects the user to a 24-hour emergency response center.

  • Who it is for: Older adults with a high risk of falls, acute health problems, or those who live alone and spend most of their time without direct supervision.
  • Advantages:
    • 24/7 Security: Emergency professionals are on the other end of the button day or night.
    • Absolute simplicity: Only requires pressing a physical button. No touchscreen or smartphone skills needed.
    • Social check-ins: Many providers offer periodic follow-up calls to combat loneliness.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Recurring cost: Usually requires a monthly subscription fee.
    • Passive: Does not help organize or coordinate daily family caregiving tasks.
    • Stigma: Some older adults refuse to wear the pendant because they associate it with a loss of independence.

2. Communication and coordination apps (e.g., DiAlgo)

Family communication apps focus on connecting the dependent person with their immediate network of care (family members, neighbors, and professional caregivers). Instead of calling an external emergency center, they facilitate daily communication and the internal organization of care tasks.

  • Who it is for: Families who want to stay aligned on daily care, share the task load (groceries, doctor appointments, medication), and maintain constant and adapted contact with the older adult.
  • Advantages:
    • Real coordination: Avoids family misunderstandings and centralizes care data in one organized place (preventing WhatsApp group clutter).
    • Social integration: Fosters the older adult’s independence, enabling them to interact easily with children and grandchildren through adapted interfaces.
    • Free or low-cost: No expensive dedicated hardware to purchase.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Requires a device: The older adult needs access to a smartphone or tablet and internet connection.
    • Not for critical medical emergencies: If the user experiences a severe medical crisis, a coordination app does not replace emergency medical services or calling local emergency numbers (like 112 or 911).

Which one should you choose? When to combine both?

The choice depends on your relative’s level of dependency and your family support network:

  • Choose Telecare if your exclusive priority is safety from falls or medical crises, and your relative has severe difficulties using any touchscreen device.
  • Choose a Communication App (DiAlgo) if you want to improve daily family interaction, organize medication collaboratively among siblings, and keep important care notes from getting lost in WhatsApp.
  • Combine both (the most recommended option): Telecare provides 24-hour emergency peace of mind, while an app like DiAlgo allows the family to organize daily schedules, care notes, and maintain structured emotional contact.
