How to communicate with a person with dementia: Practical family guide

Practical tips and communication guidelines recommended by leading geriatric and Alzheimer's societies.

Progressive dementia alters a person’s ability to process information, express themselves, and understand others. For families and caregivers, watching communication decline with a loved one can be one of the most challenging and emotionally draining experiences.

However, adapting the way we speak and listen not only reduces mutual frustration but also preserves the emotional connection and dignity of the individual. In this guide, we compile key guidelines backed by clinical references and support organisations.

1. Prepare the environment and your attitude

Before starting any conversation, it is vital to establish a calm and trusting atmosphere:

  • Eliminate distractions: Turn off the television, radio, or close the window if there is outside noise. People with dementia have difficulty filtering secondary stimuli.
  • Maintain eye contact: Always approach from the front. Get down to their eye level (sit down if the person is in a wheelchair or armchair) and establish eye contact before you begin to speak.
  • Positive body language: Your posture, smile, and tone of voice convey more information than words. A calm, slow, and affectionate tone provides security.

2. Guidelines for speaking clearly

How we structure our sentences makes a significant difference in their ability to understand:

  • Speak slowly and clearly: Use short, simple sentences. Avoid infantilising language or talking about them as if they were not present.
  • One idea at a time: Do not chain questions or complex instructions. For example, instead of saying “Let’s get dressed, then we’ll have breakfast and after that we’ll go to the doctor”, divide the action: “Let’s put on the shirt”.
  • Closed questions: Facilitate responses by asking questions that can be answered with a “yes” or “no”, or offering choices between two clear options. For example: “Do you prefer the blue cup or the red one?” instead of “What would you like for breakfast today?”.
  • Use real names: Avoid confusing pronouns. Say “Your son Michael is calling” instead of “He is calling”.

3. Active listening and empathy

Communication is a two-way street. Listening patiently is as important as speaking clearly:

  • Allow time to respond: A person with dementia may take several seconds (even a minute) to process what they have heard and formulate a reply. Avoid interrupting or finishing sentences for them unless strictly necessary.
  • Validate feelings, do not argue: If your family member insists on a distorted reality (for example, that they want to go to their deceased parents’ house), do not argue or try to impose logical reality. It is more effective to validate the underlying emotion (“You miss your parents, don’t you? Tell me about them”) and then gently redirect the conversation.
  • Pay attention to non-verbal cues: Gestures, restlessness, or facial expressions can indicate pain, thirst, discomfort, or the need to use the restroom when words are no longer accessible.

4. The value of mutual support and adapted technology

As dementia progresses, coordination among family members becomes crucial to ensure that everyone uses consistent communication guidelines and is aware of the dependent person’s status.

Simple shared family tools, like DiAlgo, help centralise daily notes, mood tracking, and communication patterns that work best. This way, both family members and external caregivers can maintain a coherent and caring approach without overwhelming the patient.
